🎧 UNIt 1 Listening script: ‘MindTalk Radio’

Host (Maya):
Welcome back to MindTalk Radio, the programme where we explore ideas from psychology in a simple and practical way. Today’s topic is something many young people think about: the connection between our personal identity and the idols or role models we choose. Why do we admire certain people? And how does this influence the way we see ourselves? To help us understand this, we have Dr. Lewis, a specialist in youth psychology. Welcome, Doctor!
Dr. Lewis: Thank you, Maya. It’s great to be here!
Maya: Let’s start with something basic. Many listeners ask: How does a role model actually affect someone’s identity?
Dr. Lewis: That’s a wonderful question! In psychology, we say that a role model gives us a kind of mental guide. When we admire someone, we pay attention to their behaviour, their attitude, and even their habits. Sometimes we start copying these things without realising it. This copying is called positive modelling, and it can help people develop important life skills.
Maya:So admiring someone can actually help us grow?
Dr. Lewis: Absolutely. For example, if your role model is a musician who practices every day, you might learn the value of discipline. Or if your idol is a scientist who is always asking questions, you may become more curious or more analytical. These qualities become part of your self-image, the way you understand who you are.
Maya: That makes sense. But are there any risks? Can choosing the wrong role model harm a person’s identity?
Dr. Lewis: Yes, it can. A healthy idol inspires you to be better. But a harmful idol might encourage behaviours that are unsafe or unrealistic. Young people sometimes admire celebrities who show a perfect life online. This can create pressure, and people start comparing themselves in unhealthy ways. Instead of learning confidence, they feel self-doubt. That’s why it’s important to choose role models who show realistic, positive qualities.
Maya: Can a person have more than one role model?
Dr. Lewis: Of course. In fact, it’s quite helpful. One person might inspire your creativity, another your work ethic, and another your emotional strength. Our identity is not one single thing—it is a combination of many influences. Having several positive figures helps you build a more balanced sense of who you want to be.
Maya: What about family members? Are they still important as role models?
Dr. Lewis: Very much so. Many people first learn about responsibility, patience, or kindness from parents, grandparents, or older siblings. Even if you admire famous people, the everyday role models in your family often have the strongest impact, because you actually see their actions in real life.
Maya: Before we finish, can you give our listeners one message to remember?
Dr. Lewis: Sure. Your identity is something you create step by step. Choose idols who encourage growth, not perfection. Follow people who show integrity, effort, and emotional balance. And most importantly, remember that your identity should be based on your own choices, not on pressure from others.
Maya: Thank you, Dr. Lewis. And thank you to our listeners. This has been MindTalk Radio. Join us next week when we explore how daily routines shape our mental health. Have a great day!
🎧 Unit 2 Listening Script: Academic Success Seminar


Good morning, everyone, and welcome to our Academic Success Seminar. Today, we are talking about how to learn better. We will look at different learning styles, useful study strategies, and simple techniques that can help you succeed in your classes.

People learn in different ways. Some students learn best by seeing information. These are visual learners. They understand ideas better when they look at pictures, charts, colours, or written notes.

Other students learn best by hearing. These are auditory learners. They remember information well when they listen to explanations, discussions, or recordings.

There are also kinaesthetic learners. These students like to learn by doing things. They enjoy hands-on activities, experiments, or moving while they study. Most people are a mix of these styles.

One helpful strategy is active learning. This means interacting with the material—asking questions, explaining ideas to someone else, or writing short summaries.

Another strategy is spaced practice. This means studying a little bit every day instead of studying everything the night before a test.

Setting goals is also important. Clear goals help you stay focused and motivated.

Some useful techniques include the Pomodoro method—studying for 25 minutes and taking a 5-minute break, and using flashcards for vocabulary or definitions.

Finally, healthy habits like getting enough sleep, drinking water, and keeping your study space clean will help you learn more effectively.

Thank you for listening, and good luck with your studies!

Unit 4   LISTENING SCRIPT the characters in paintings


Today, you will hear about four of the most famous paintings in the world. Each painting shows a different person or character, and each one has an interesting story behind it. Although these artworks were created hundreds of years apart, they continue to inspire people everywhere.
Let’s begin with “The Starry Night” by Vincent van Gogh.
This painting does not show a person, but the village itself becomes the character of the artwork. The small houses, quiet church tower, and tall dark tree seem alive under the swirling sky. Van Gogh painted this scene in 1889 while staying in a hospital in the south of France. From his window, he imagined a night sky filled with movement and emotion. The character of this painting is the dreamlike world Van Gogh created — a world full of strong feelings, bright colours, and deep imagination.
The second painting is “Mona Lisa” by Leonardo da Vinci.
The main character in this painting is a woman with a calm face and a mysterious smile. Many historians believe she was Lisa Gherardini, a woman from Florence. Leonardo used soft colours and gentle shading to make her look real and full of life. Her expression changes depending on where you stand, and this makes her one of the most famous characters in art history. The “Mona Lisa” represents quiet confidence and inner calm, and millions of visitors come to see her at the Louvre Museum in Paris.
Next is “Girl with a Pearl Earring” by Johannes Vermeer.
The girl in this painting is not a famous person. Many experts believe she may have been a local girl who modelled for Vermeer. The character is known for her gentle expression, bright eyes, and the shiny pearl earring that catches the light. She wears a blue and yellow turban, which was unusual for the time. Her slight turn of the head makes it feel like she is about to speak. This painting is often called “the Dutch Mona Lisa” because of her quiet but powerful presence.
Finally, let’s look at “The Scream” by Edvard Munch.
The character in this painting is a figure standing on a bridge, holding their face in fear. The sky behind them is bright red and orange, creating a feeling of tension. Munch wrote that he once felt “a great scream in nature,” and the painting shows that moment of emotion. The character represents anxiety, stress, or fear, something many people understand today. The Scream has become a symbol of modern emotional struggles.
All four artworks show different characters — a dreamlike village, a mysterious woman, a quiet young girl, and a frightened figure. Their stories remind us that art can express human feelings in powerful ways.
🎧 unit 5 Listening Script:“Don’t Judge a Book by Its movie…”


Today, we often decide what to watch or read based on short online comments or quick photos we see on the internet. Because of this, many people believe they already know a story before they actually experience it. However, when a written story is turned into a film, things can change in surprising ways. This is why many readers say, “Don’t judge a book by its film.”
Let’s look at a few world-famous stories that reached global audiences through both pages and screens. They show us how two ways of telling a story can feel completely different.
First, there is the magical tale Harry Potter, read by millions of people around the world. The written version takes readers deep into a world of learning, friendship, and courage. The film versions also became extremely popular, but many readers noticed that some moments were shorter or missing. For example, certain conversations and small details about the school never appeared on screen. People who only watched the films often said, “I didn’t know this happened,” when they later read the books. This reminds us that one version can never include everything.
Another great example is The Hunger Games. The written version gives us long descriptions of the main character’s thoughts, worries, and hopes. When it was adapted into a film, the story became more action-focused. It was exciting, but the inner voice of the hero felt quieter. Readers often say that the book helped them understand the emotions behind the dangerous events, while the film made the experience much faster and more visual.
Let’s take The Lord of the Rings, a classic loved across generations. The books are very detailed, with long passages about the world, its landscapes, and the history behind everything. The films, however, chose a different style. They focused on key journeys and left out many smaller parts. Some fans enjoyed this because it helped the story move forward quickly. Others felt that important ideas were missing. This shows how filmmakers must make difficult choices when adapting long stories.
A very different example is Life of Pi, a story about survival, belief, and imagination. Many people found the book peaceful and slow, giving them time to think about the meaning behind the events. The film impressed viewers with bright colours and powerful scenes, but some readers said it felt less personal. The message was still there, but it reached the audience in a different way.
Finally, The Diary of Anne Frank offers another lesson. The written diary feels private and honest, as if the writer is speaking directly to us. When it was turned into a film, the events remained touching, but the feeling of reading real personal thoughts was difficult to recreate.
These examples teach us something important: a book and its film are not rivals—they are different experiences. One invites us into the writer’s mind, while the other invites us to see events with our own eyes. Both can be powerful, but they will never be exactly the same.
So next time someone says, “I watched the movie, so I don’t need to read the book,” you can smile and answer, “Don’t judge a book by its film… or you might miss half the story.”

The other person has an unlimited bank account, meaning they can book anything they want: first-class flights, five-star hotels, private tours, and luxury restaurants.

Many students who watch the show say it helps them understand how to plan their own day trips, think about travel insurance, and choose between budget travel and more comfortable options.
“Budget vs Luxury Travel” reminds us that there is no single right way to explore the world. What matters is the experience you create and the memories you take with you.
